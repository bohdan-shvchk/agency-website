## Prime Video Joins the Short-Form Video Wars With New 'Clips' Feed

Prime Video is officially jumping into the short-form video trend, following Netflix and Disney with a TikTok-like Clips feed in its app. The feature aims to solve the discovery problem that plagues longer-form streaming content by offering users a scrollable feed of bite-sized snippets from shows and movies. This move represents a significant shift in how streaming platforms are rethinking engagement as they battle for user attention in an increasingly fragmented entertainment landscape.

The Clips feed strategy is becoming industry standard as Netflix's success with similar features has proven the model works. By lowering the barrier to entry—users don't need to commit to watching a full episode—streaming giants hope to increase session time and surface content that might otherwise go undiscovered. For Prime Video, this is particularly important as the platform aims to keep pace with competitors who have already built loyal short-form audiences.

## Intel's Stock Soars 490%, But Wall Street May Be Getting Ahead of Itself

Intel's stock has experienced a stunning 490% rise over the past year, marking one of the most dramatic comebacks in tech history. However, analysts are cautioning that this rally may be pricing in a turnaround that hasn't fully materialized yet. The semiconductor giant's resurgence is built on promises of new manufacturing capacity and architectural improvements, but execution risks remain significant.

The stock surge reflects investor optimism about Intel's foundry business and its ability to compete with TSMC and Samsung. However, the company still faces substantial headwinds including delayed product launches and massive capital expenditure requirements. Wall Street's bet suggests confidence in CEO Pat Gelsinger's strategy, but skeptics warn that current valuations may not leave much room for disappointment.

## Cloudflare Cuts 1,100 Jobs as AI Drives Historic Efficiency Gains

In a stark demonstration of AI's impact on workforce dynamics, Cloudflare announced its first large-scale layoff affecting 1,100 employees—even as the company reported record-high revenue. CEO Matthew Prince attributed the cuts directly to AI efficiency gains, stating the company no longer needs the same level of headcount to support operations and serve customers. The move signals a turning point where AI-driven productivity is translating directly into workforce reductions at major tech companies.

Cloudflare's layoffs represent one of the most explicit admissions yet that AI is making jobs obsolete across the tech industry. The company cited improvements in support automation, operations, and development as key drivers. This trend is likely to accelerate as more companies realize they can maintain or grow revenue while significantly reducing their payroll, raising questions about the broader labor market impact of AI adoption.

## Porsche Refocuses Strategy, Shuttering E-Bike, Battery, and Software Units

Porsche is undergoing a significant strategic overhaul, shuttering its e-bike, battery, and software subsidiaries as the automotive manufacturer refocuses on its core business. The closures will affect more than 500 employees as the company reassesses its diversification efforts. CEO Michael Leiters emphasized that the restructuring is necessary to concentrate resources where Porsche has the most competitive advantage: building premium vehicles.

The move reflects a broader trend among traditional manufacturers reconsidering their AI and tech ambitions. While Porsche invested in adjacent markets hoping to capture emerging opportunities, the company has determined that managing multiple business units dilutes focus and drains resources. This consolidation strategy prioritizes profitability and operational excellence over diversification.

## Polish Intelligence Warns of Russian Hacking Campaign Targeting Water Treatment Plants

Poland's top intelligence agency has accused Russia of conducting a sophisticated sabotage and hacking campaign against the country's military and civilian infrastructure, with particular focus on water treatment plants. The breach represents a critical vulnerability in essential services and signals a troubling trend: the U.S. is reportedly facing identical threats to its own water infrastructure. These attacks underscore how critical infrastructure has become a primary target for state-sponsored cyber operations.

The investigation revealed that Russian-linked threat actors successfully breached multiple Polish facilities, with the potential for physical damage or service disruption. U.S. officials have warned American water utilities to increase security measures and threat monitoring. The incident highlights the inadequate cybersecurity posture of many critical infrastructure operators and the urgent need for government-mandated security standards and funding.

## NHTSA Investigates Uber Partner Avride Over Self-Driving Crashes

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration has opened an investigation into Avride, Uber's autonomous vehicle partner, following more than a dozen crashes and at least one minor injury. The probe comes as autonomous vehicle companies face increased scrutiny over safety records and incident reporting. Avride's safety track record is now under the microscope as regulators determine whether the company's autonomous systems pose an unreasonable risk to the public.

The investigation represents a critical moment for the autonomous vehicle industry as it scales operations. Even minor incidents are drawing regulatory attention, and companies must demonstrate robust safety protocols and transparent reporting. This scrutiny could impact the timeline for broader AV deployment and influence how other autonomous operators manage their programs.
